## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending June 22 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **TAO at +0.11% net** on the forward view — gross block emission of **0.324M TAO** almost entirely cancelled by a registration recycle of **0.313M TAO**. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day change at **+0.12%**, a gap of about **0.01 percentage points** — well inside tolerance, so **no monitor-gap chip** ships. Bittensor is a **structurally near-flat, hard-capped halving asset**: the mint is real, but recycling delays issuance and holds circulating supply almost steady.

## Sell pressure: where new TAO comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is the whole story, at about **0.324M TAO** over the next 90 days. Bittensor mints new TAO every block on a fixed, Bitcoin-style halving schedule. The first halving fired on **Dec 14 2025**, cutting emission from 1 TAO per block to **0.5 TAO per block**, or roughly **3,600 TAO per day**. That issuance is split across miners, validators and subnet owners, but every slice is newly created supply, so it counts once, here. The next halving fires only when total issued reaches 15.75M TAO — years away and outside this window — so the rate is constant across the 90 days.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is **zero**: Bittensor fair-launched with no presale, team or investor allocation, so there is no vesting cliff and no locked tranche to reach the market. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is also zero, because there is no large team-controlled stockpile and no dated discretionary release pending. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, since no bankruptcy estate or court distribution applies to TAO.

## Buy pressure: where new TAO goes

Buy #2 — the registration recycle — is the dominant offset, at about **0.313M TAO** over 90 days. On Bittensor, TAO spent on subnet and miner registration is converted to alpha and returned to the **unissued supply** — removed from circulation and pushing the next halving further out. Because registration demand scales with network activity, the recycle absorbs nearly all of the block emission, leaving circulating supply almost flat. The framework books this on the buy side because it is a structural removal of TAO from the float, even though it is a recycle rather than a permanent burn.

Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is **zero**: Bittensor runs no revenue-funded purchase of TAO on the open market. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — is zero, with no discretionary project buying observed. Buy #4 — new long-term lock — is zero, with no new escrow or multi-year lock announced in the window. The recycle is the only meaningful counter-flow, and it is large enough to neutralize the mint.

## Foundation and overhang

TAO has no classic unlock overhang. Because Bittensor fair-launched with no presale or team allocation, there is no investor cliff, no foundation tranche and no scheduled release waiting in the wings — the only ongoing source of new supply is the protocol mint itself, captured in Sell #1. The closest thing to a structural lever is the recycle pool: registration fees flow back into unissued supply, and that flow rises and falls with how aggressively miners and subnet owners register. The framework re-checks emission and recycle on a roughly bi-weekly walk; if a discretionary release ever appeared, the outflow would enter Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How TAO compares to other hard-capped halving chains

TAO belongs to the class of **hard-capped, halving-model assets** — the same structural family as Bitcoin, with a fixed 21M ceiling and emission that halves on schedule. What sets Bittensor apart is the recycle: where Bitcoin permanently mints to miners and lets the float grow, Bittensor returns registration spend to the unissued supply, so realized circulating growth can sit far below the gross mint. That makes TAO read as near-flat on the active float even while it is technically still issuing.

Against uncapped continuous-emission L1s, the contrast is sharper still. Those chains dilute holders indefinitely; TAO has a ceiling and, thanks to the recycle, is barely diluting at the current pace. Against exchange tokens that go net-deflationary through revenue-funded burns, TAO is different in kind — it has no buyback and no permanent burn, so it does not shrink supply; it simply holds it nearly steady while the halving schedule slowly tightens issuance further.

## What to watch in the next 90 days

Watch the recycle rate — registration demand is the single variable that decides whether net inflation stays near zero or drifts up if recycling slows. Watch total issued creeping toward the 15.75M halving mark; it is still years out, but the live trackers update it continuously. Watch subnet growth, since more subnets mean more registration spend and a larger recycle. And expect the framework to keep tracking our supply monitor closely, because the recycle is a genuine circulating removal that both readings capture — the two should stay within a fraction of a point.

TAO is a hard-capped, halving-model token whose supply is technically still issuing but barely growing. The key risk is recycle slowing; the key constraint is the 21M hard cap and a halving schedule that only tightens issuance from here.
