## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window starting June 22 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **ETC at +0.30% net** on the forward view, driven entirely by proof-of-work block-reward issuance with nothing on the buy side. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day change at **+0.31%**, essentially identical — a gap of about **0.0 percentage points**, so **no monitor-gap chip** ships. The agreement is structural: ETC has no vesting, no burn and no buyback, so the gross block reward is the whole story, and the chain's actual minting is exactly what the framework counts. Ethereum Classic is a **slowly, mildly inflationary capped-supply chain** — closer to neutral than to a high-emission token.

## Sell pressure: where new ETC comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is the whole story, at about **0.46M ETC** over the next 90 days. Ethereum Classic is proof-of-work: new ETC is paid to miners as the block reward, currently **2.048 ETC** per block. That reward follows the **5M20** monetary policy set in ECIP-1017 — it drops **20%** every 5,000,000 blocks, a "Fifthening" that is ETC's answer to Bitcoin's halving. The next cut, to **1.6384 ETC** per block at block 25,000,001, falls inside this window around **early September 2026**, so most of the 90 days mints at the old rate and a short tail mints at the lower one — which is why forward issuance reads slightly below the trailing window.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is **zero**: ETC has no team, seed or treasury vesting. The chain carries the original Ethereum distribution, which has been fully in circulation for years, so there is no cliff to hit the market. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is also zero; the Olympia on-chain treasury that will one day collect base-fee revenue is a late-2026 upgrade that does not release coins and has no balance to distribute in this window. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, because no bankruptcy estate or court distribution applies to ETC.

## Buy pressure: where new ETC goes

Every buy row is **zero** — Ethereum Classic has no mechanism that removes coins from supply. Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is zero: no protocol or foundation spends to buy ETC back off the market. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is also zero: ETC does not burn transaction fees today. The Olympia upgrade will add an EIP-1559-style base fee in late 2026, but unlike Ethereum it **redirects** that fee to an on-chain treasury rather than burning it, so even after launch there is no burn. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — and Buy #4 — new long-term lock — are both zero, with no discretionary open-market buying or new escrow announced. With nothing on the buy side, the block reward is unopposed, and net inflation is simply the gross mint divided by circulating supply.

## Foundation and overhang

ETC has **no team-controlled overhang** to track — there is no foundation treasury wallet on a release schedule, no vesting contract, no buyback wallet accumulating coins, and no bankruptcy residual. The token is fully distributed and the only new supply is freshly mined ETC paid to miners block by block. The one structure worth naming for the future is the **Olympia treasury**: once live, it will accrue redirected base-fee revenue under DAO governance, but it mints no new coins and, as a late-2026 upgrade, holds no balance in this window. If that treasury ever begins distributing ETC to fund proposals, those outflows would enter Sell #3 at the next refresh; until then there is nothing to book.

## How ETC compares to other proof-of-work capped-supply chains

ETC belongs to the class of **proof-of-work coins with a hard-capped, step-down emission schedule** — the same family as Bitcoin, but with a gentler curve. Where Bitcoin halves the reward every roughly four years, ETC's 5M20 policy cuts the reward 20% every 5,000,000 blocks, about every two and a half years. The result is a smoother, more gradual approach to the ceiling: ETC's theoretical cap is about **210.7M** coins, and with roughly 156.6M already mined, the curve is well past its steepest phase and issuing slowly.

Against uncapped proof-of-stake L1s with continuous emission, ETC looks restrained — its supply is bounded and its issuance only ever falls. Against fee-burning chains like Ethereum, the contrast is the opposite: ETC has no burn, so it cannot go net-deflationary the way a high-activity burn chain can. For an inflation lens specifically, that places ETC in the middle — a low, positive issuance rate that steps down on schedule, with no offsetting burn to pull it negative. The dominant force is the decaying block reward; the brake is the calendar, not a buyback.

## What to watch in the next 90 days

Watch the 5th Fifthening around **early September 2026** — the block-reward cut at block 25,000,001 lowers issuance 20%, the single event that moves the framework reading this window. Watch the realized block time, since ETC's issuance pace tracks how fast miners actually produce blocks; a faster or slower chain shifts both the gross mint and the exact date the reduction fires. Watch the Olympia upgrade timeline — if the EIP-1559 base fee and treasury ship earlier than expected, the framework re-checks whether the redirected fee is burned or merely diverted. And note that for as long as ETC has no burn and no buyback, the framework and our supply monitor will keep agreeing closely — any divergence would be the signal to look for a new mechanism.
