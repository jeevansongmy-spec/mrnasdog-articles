## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending June 16 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **VET at 0.00% net**. There is no sell pressure and no buy pressure on the VET ledger, because VET's supply is fixed: it was set at the 2017 launch and has not changed. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day change at **−0.0001%** — a gap of about **0.00 percentage points**, comfortably inside tolerance, so **no ⚠ monitor-gap chip** ships. VET is a **quiet, fixed-supply token**: the entire inflation story sits one layer away, in VTHO.

## Sell pressure: where new VET comes from

Nowhere — Sell #1, protocol inflation, is **0**. VET is not mined and not minted; the supply was fixed at launch and the chain never issues more. The reward token that VeChain holders earn is VTHO, a second token generated by holding and now staking VET, and partly burned to pay transaction fees. Crucially, VTHO is issued and burned on its own ledger — those flows change the VTHO count, never the VET count, so they do not belong in this column.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is **0**: every original team, seed and investor allocation finished vesting long ago, so no cliff reaches the market in this window. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is also **0** as a flow; the foundation holds a treasury of VET that it reports each quarter, but there is no scheduled or dated discretionary release inside the window. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is **0**, because no bankruptcy estate or court distribution applies to VET.

## Buy pressure: where new VET goes

Also nowhere — every buy row is **0**. Buy #1, programmatic buyback, is zero: there is no VET buyback-and-burn. Buy #2, protocol fee burn, is the one most people misread — VeChain does burn fees, about 70% of every transaction fee, but those fees are paid and burned in **VTHO**, not VET. That burn shrinks VTHO supply and has no effect on the VET count, so on the VET ledger it is zero.

Buy #3, Foundation buy, is zero — no discretionary open-market VET buying was announced in the window. Buy #4, new long-term lock, is zero in the sense that matters here: the December 2025 upgrade introduced an NFT-based staking platform where holders lock VET to earn VTHO, and several billion VET are now staked, but staked VET still counts as circulating supply. A lock that keeps coins in the float does not remove them from it, so it does not register as buy pressure.

## Foundation and overhang

VET has no classic unlock overhang — the original allocations are long distributed. The one team-controlled balance worth naming is the foundation treasury, a multi-hundred-million-dollar pool of VET and other assets that the foundation discloses in quarterly transparency reports and spends slowly on grants, partnerships and operations. It is not a stockpile on a release schedule; recent quarters show only modest outflow. The framework books no discretionary release beyond what those reports show, and re-checks the treasury disclosures and on-chain balances on a roughly bi-weekly walk. If the treasury balance falls faster than the reports imply, the outflow enters Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How VET compares to other fixed-supply chains

VET belongs to the class of **fixed-supply Layer-1 tokens with a separate gas asset** — its closest structural cousin is a coin whose total was set once and never grows. Unlike an uncapped proof-of-stake chain that mints fresh tokens to pay validators, VeChain pays its validators in a second token, VTHO, so the staking-and-rewards machinery runs entirely outside VET's supply. That dual-token design is the whole reason VET reads flat: the inflation that a normal chain bakes into its base token is exported to the gas token instead.

Against a hard-capped, halving-model coin, VET is similar in that supply does not grow, but different in mechanism: there is no mining emission to taper, because there was never any emission to begin with. Against an exchange token that burns its way to deflation, VET is again different — its burn is in VTHO, so VET neither inflates nor deflates. For an inflation lens specifically, that makes VET one of the rare assets that is genuinely, structurally neutral on supply: the number of VET in existence simply does not move.

## What to watch in the next 90 days

Watch the foundation's next quarterly transparency report — it is the single item that could turn the flat Sell #3 into a real number if treasury VET were sold in size. Watch governance for any proposal that touches the VET supply itself, which would be a structural change rather than a routine flow. Note the continued roll-out of the new staking platform: it locks more VET for holders and reshapes VTHO emission, but unless a future change reclassifies staked VET out of circulating supply, it leaves the VET count flat. And keep an eye on the gas token VTHO separately — that is where VeChain's actual inflation and burn dynamics live.

## Summary

VET is a fixed-supply token: about 85.99B coins, set at the 2017 launch, with no mint and no burn, so the framework reads 0.00% net and our supply monitor agrees at −0.0001%. The inflation people associate with VeChain — and the well-known fee burn — both belong to the separate gas token VTHO, which is generated by staking VET and burned to pay fees without ever changing the VET count. The key risk is not dilution but discretionary treasury selling, which would show up in the foundation's quarterly reports. On supply, VET is as neutral as a token gets — flat, by design, with the active inflation exported one layer away.
