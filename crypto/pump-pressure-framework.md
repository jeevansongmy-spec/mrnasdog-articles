## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending June 16 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **PUMP at about +18% net** on the forward view. PUMP is the token of the Solana launchpad pump.fun; it has a **fixed one-trillion supply cap** and no protocol inflation, so the only forces on supply are scheduled vesting unlocks and a fee-funded buyback-burn. Over the next 90 days the burn removes roughly 18B PUMP, but the July 12 cliff adds about 82.5B — so float rises sharply this window. This is the mirror image of the prior 90 days, when a record one-time burn cut circulating supply by about 41%. PUMP is therefore **structurally a swing token**: deeply deflationary when buybacks pile up and burn, sharply inflationary when a big lockup expires.

## Sell pressure: where new PUMP comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is **zero**. PUMP is capped at one trillion and has no staking or block mint, so the protocol never creates new coins on its own. Every coin that will ever exist already exists; supply only moves as locked allocations vest into the float.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is the whole sell story this window, at about **82.5B PUMP**. The one-year lockup on the team and early-investor allocations expires on **July 12 2026**, releasing roughly 50B to the team and 32.5B to investors in a single cliff — close to a quarter of today's circulating supply landing at once. After the cliff, both buckets begin a multi-year linear vesting, but the dated cliff is the event that defines the forward window. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is zero, with no discretionary release pending. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero.

## Buy pressure: where PUMP goes

Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is the only active offset, at about **18B PUMP** over 90 days. Since late April 2026, half of all net revenue from the bonding curve, PumpSwap and the Terminal is automatically used to buy PUMP on the open market and burn it immediately, locked into an irreversible one-year contract that runs without team discretion. At the current revenue pace — about $18.85M a month — that works out to roughly $28M of buying over 90 days, or about 18B coins destroyed for good. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is zero as a separate line: there is no base-fee burn, the revenue buyback is the only burn, and it is already counted above. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — and Buy #4 — new long-term lock — are both zero.

## The April burn and the monitor gap

The prior 90 days tell the opposite story. On **April 29 2026**, pump.fun executed a one-time mega-burn that cleared roughly nine months of accumulated buybacks — about 36% of circulating supply, cutting the float from around 590B to roughly 332B in a single day. That is why our supply monitor reads the last-90-day change at **−41%**. The framework, measuring the same drop against today's smaller 347.7B circulating base, reads **−70%** for that window. Both numbers describe the identical token change of about 242.5B coins; the difference is only which base the percentage divides by — the monitor uses the window's starting supply, the framework uses today's. Because the gap is a denominator convention and not a missing flow, the primary read is kept and the page ships a **⚠ monitor-gap chip** to flag it honestly.

## Foundation and overhang

PUMP's overhang is unusually concentrated. The team (20% of supply) and early investors (13%) sat under a one-year lockup that all expires on the same July 12 date, so a large share of the total supply moves from fully locked to vesting in one step. That is the single biggest risk to the float over the next year, and it is exactly why the forward read flips inflationary despite an aggressive burn. The framework books the cliff as a dated event and will track how fast the unlocked coins reach the market; the post-cliff linear vesting adds a steady trickle on top. The buyback-burn is the structural brake, but at roughly 18B per 90 days it cannot offset an 82.5B cliff inside a single window.

## How PUMP compares to other capped, buyback-burn tokens

PUMP belongs to the class of **fixed-cap tokens with a revenue-funded buyback-burn** — closer to an exchange token than to an inflation chain. Unlike an uncapped proof-of-stake coin, PUMP can never mint; unlike a passive fixed-supply token, it actively destroys coins out of revenue. What makes it unusual is the size and timing of its unlocks: most buyback-burn tokens fight a slow, steady emission, but PUMP fights a small number of very large cliffs. The result is a token that whipsaws between deflation and inflation depending on which side fires that quarter.

The contrast worth drawing is with a token that burns continuously and unlocks continuously — there, the two forces roughly net out month to month. PUMP does not have that smoothness. Its burn is continuous but modest, and its unlocks are lumpy and huge, so any single 90-day read is dominated by whether a cliff lands inside it. For an inflation lens, that means PUMP's headline can swing from −41% to +18% in back-to-back windows without anything being wrong in the math.

## What to watch in the next 90 days

Watch the July 12 cliff first — whether the unlocked team and investor coins actually sell or sit, since the 82.5B is the number that decides the window. Watch platform revenue, because the buyback-burn is fixed at 50% of it, so a stronger launchpad quarter means a bigger burn to lean against the unlock. Watch that the burn contract holds its commitment through its one-year term. And expect the framework to keep reading on a different base than our supply monitor for as long as the April burn sits inside the trailing window — that gap is a denominator convention, not a new flow.
