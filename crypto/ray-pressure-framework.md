Raydium mints only about **0.5M RAY** over 90 days from a shrinking mining reserve, while a buyback funded by **12%** of all trading fees takes roughly **1.2M RAY** off the open market and holds it. The buyback edges out the mint, so the Pressure Framework reads about **−0.3% net** on the float. Our supply monitor reads **+0.15%** — essentially flat — and the two agree inside tolerance.

## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending June 16 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **RAY at about −0.3% net** on the forward view: the fee-funded buyback removes slightly more RAY from the open market than the mining reserve issues. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day change at **+0.15%** — flat — versus the framework's **−0.26%** read. That is a gap of about **0.4 percentage points**, inside the 0.5-point tolerance, so **no monitor-gap chip ships**. The agreement is structural: the buyback wallet is treated as non-circulating, so coins bought back leave the float at roughly the rate the reserve adds to it. RAY is best read as **structurally flat-to-slightly-deflationary on the active float**.

## Sell pressure: where new RAY comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is small and shrinking, at about **0.5M RAY** over the next 90 days. Raydium's original three-year emission schedule front-loaded **74%** of the mining reserve into its first year and finished in 2024; what remains is a thin tail of farming and staking rewards drawn from the reserve at roughly **1.9M RAY a year**. That is the only genuinely new RAY entering the market.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is **zero**: every team, seed, advisor and ecosystem allocation finished vesting in 2024, so RAY is fully unlocked and no cliff hits the market. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is also zero as a flow; about **286M RAY** sits outside circulation across the mining reserve, the treasury, and the buyback wallet, but none of it has a published release schedule. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, because no bankruptcy estate or court distribution applies to RAY.

## Buy pressure: where new RAY goes

Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is the dominant force, at about **1.2M RAY** over 90 days. Raydium routes **12%** of all trading fees into automatic RAY purchases on the open market. The critical detail is the destination — bought-back RAY is sent to a public accumulation wallet and **held, not burned**. The protocol's treasury closed Q3 2025 with its RAY position near **$189M**, about 79% of treasury assets, confirming the hold. Because the coins leave the open float even though they are not destroyed, they count as buy-side pressure here.

Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is **zero**, because there is no burn: the buyback accumulates RAY in a wallet rather than destroying it. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — and Buy #4 — new long-term lock — are both zero, with no discretionary buying outside the fee buyback and no new escrow announced in the window.

## Foundation and overhang

The team-controlled overhang for RAY is the roughly **286M RAY** that sits outside circulation. It breaks into three tracked buckets: the remaining mining reserve that funds the thin emission tail; the protocol treasury; and the buyback accumulation wallet, which has gathered about **72M RAY** (roughly 27% of the circulating float) at a cumulative cost above $200M since the program began. None carries a published release schedule, so each is booked at zero flow and watched rather than projected. If any of these balances falls between refreshes, the outflow enters Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How RAY compares to other DEX and exchange tokens

RAY belongs to the class of **fee-sharing DEX tokens with a hard cap and a revenue-funded buyback**. Unlike an uncapped proof-of-stake L1 that mints continuously, RAY is capped at **555M** and almost fully issued, so dilution risk is structurally low. The closest analogues are exchange and DEX tokens that recycle a share of fees into buybacks — but the key mechanism split is what happens to the coins afterward.

Some exchange tokens **burn** what they buy, which permanently shrinks total supply; Raydium instead **holds** bought-back RAY in a treasury wallet. For an inflation lens the practical effect over a single window is similar — the coins leave the open float either way — but the long-run read differs: a hold can in principle be reversed by governance, whereas a burn cannot. That is why RAY reads as flat-to-slightly-deflationary on the active float rather than aggressively deflationary: the float shrinks, but total supply does not.

## What to watch in the next 90 days

Watch the buyback run-rate — it has fallen sharply from 15.4M RAY in Q1 2025 to about 2M RAY in Q3 2025 as DEX volumes cooled, and since the buyback is 12% of trading fees, a weak quarter shrinks the only real offset. Watch DEX trading volume on Solana directly, as it drives the fee pool that funds everything. Watch for any governance move on the buyback wallet, since a decision to burn the held RAY — or to redeploy it — would change the read overnight. And note that the mining-reserve tail keeps thinning each year.

## Summary

RAY is a hard-capped, fully-unlocked DEX token whose new supply is now just a thin mining-reserve tail of about 0.5M RAY over 90 days. Against it, a buyback funded by 12% of trading fees takes roughly 1.2M RAY off the open market and holds it in a treasury wallet, leaving the framework at about −0.3% net. Our supply monitor reads +0.15% — flat — and the two agree inside tolerance. The key risk is the buyback run-rate, which tracks DEX volume; the key ceiling is the 555M cap, almost fully reached.
