Stellar creates no new XLM — the protocol's old 1% yearly inflation was switched off by validator vote in November 2019, and total supply has been fixed near **50.00B** ever since. Yet circulating supply still grows, because the Stellar Development Foundation moves coins from its mandate stash into the market — roughly **905M XLM** over the last 90 days. With no buyback and no burn to offset it, the Pressure Framework reads about **+2.66%** net, matched closely by our supply monitor at **+2.78%**.

## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending Jul 7 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **XLM at +2.66% net** on the forward view, driven entirely by Stellar Development Foundation distributions, with no offsetting buyback or burn. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day circulating change at **+2.78%**, versus the framework's **+2.66%** for the same window — a gap of only about **0.12 percentage points**, well inside tolerance, so no monitor-gap flag ships. The two readings agree because the Foundation release the framework books in Sell #3 is the same circulating-supply growth the monitor measures. XLM is **structurally inflationary by Foundation distribution**, at a low-single-digit pace, even though the protocol itself mints nothing.

## Sell pressure: where new XLM comes from

The whole story sits in Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — at about **905M XLM** over 90 days. The Stellar Development Foundation holds a large mandate stash of XLM across four accounts — Development, Stellar Growth, Product and Innovation, and Assets and Liquidity — that summed to about **15.72B** on Jul 6 2026, down from roughly **16.66B** at the end of March 2026. As the Foundation funds grants, ecosystem programs and operations, those coins leave custody and enter the freely-tradable float. That movement, not any protocol mint, is what lifts circulating supply — about 905M XLM reached the market over the last 90 days, and the framework projects the same observed run-rate forward.

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is **zero**: Stellar's 1% yearly mint was disabled by a validator vote in November 2019, when about 55B XLM was also burned, and total supply has stayed fixed near 50.00B XLM since, so no new coins are created. Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is also zero, because XLM has no investor, team or seed vesting schedule cliffing coins into the market. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, with no bankruptcy estate or court-ordered distribution applying to XLM.

## Buy pressure: where new XLM goes

There is no buy-side offset on XLM today. Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is **zero**: Stellar runs no buyback, and no protocol or Foundation mechanism purchases XLM on the open market. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is zero in any meaningful sense: network fees are tiny and there has been no supply-reducing burn since the one-time 2019 burn that fixed total supply near 50B. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — is zero, and the Foundation runs the opposite way: it distributes XLM outward rather than buying it back. Buy #4 — new long-term lock — is zero, with no new multi-year escrow announced in the window. With every buy row at zero, the Foundation release is unopposed, which is why the net read tracks the gross Foundation outflow almost one-for-one.

## Foundation and overhang

The overhang here is unusually clean to name: it is the Stellar Development Foundation's own mandate stash, about **15.72B XLM** as of Jul 6 2026, held across the Development, Stellar Growth, Product and Innovation, and Assets and Liquidity accounts. This is the single largest non-circulating block of XLM — roughly a third of total supply — and it drains into the float as the Foundation spends. It is not a hidden VC overhang or a dated cliff; it is a published, quarterly-reported treasury whose balance the framework re-checks on a roughly bi-weekly walk against the Foundation's mandate disclosures. If that balance falls faster than the observed run-rate between refreshes, the extra outflow enters Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How XLM compares to other fixed-supply Foundation-funded networks

XLM belongs to the class of **fixed-supply networks whose float grows by foundation distribution** rather than by protocol mint — the same shape as a coin that did a one-time supply reset and now runs on a treasury. Unlike an uncapped proof-of-stake chain that issues fresh coins to validators every block, Stellar creates nothing new; the 50B total is hard. But unlike a fully-circulating fixed-supply token, a large slice still sits in Foundation custody, so the tradable float keeps rising as that custody empties.

The contrast worth drawing is with a halving-model coin or an exchange token that burns. A halving coin's inflation is set by block reward and falls on a schedule no one controls; an exchange token can go net-deflationary when its burn outpaces issuance. XLM has neither lever — no block reward to halve and no burn to lean on. Its inflation is instead a spending decision: the Foundation's mandate pace. That makes XLM's supply path more discretionary than a halving coin's but more transparent than an opaque-treasury token, because the stash size and its quarterly drawdown are publicly reported.

And expect the framework reading and our supply monitor to keep agreeing closely, because on XLM the Foundation outflow and the circulating-supply delta are the same quantity.

XLM is a fixed-supply payment coin that mints nothing — its 1% protocol inflation was switched off in November 2019 and total supply is locked near 50.00B. Circulating supply still grows because the Stellar Development Foundation releases XLM from a ~15.72B mandate stash into the market, about 905M over the last 90 days, with no buyback or burn to offset it. The framework reads about +2.66% net, matched by our supply monitor at +2.78%, and the key risk is the Foundation's spending pace rather than any protocol mint. XLM stays mildly inflationary by distribution, capped by a hard 50B total and a Foundation stash that shrinks as it spends.
