## Sell pressure: where new DOT comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is the whole story, at about **14M DOT** over the next 90 days. Polkadot mints new DOT each era and pays it to stakers and the treasury. The decisive change came on March 14 2026 — "Pi Day" — when Referendum 1710 cut annual issuance from about **120M** to roughly **56.88M DOT**, a **53.6%** reduction that drops the yearly inflation rate to near **3.1%**. Under the new schedule, issuance keeps falling by 13.14% of the remaining supply every two years toward a hard cap.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is **zero**: Polkadot's genesis distribution and early team vesting expired years ago, so DOT is fully unlocked and no cliff hits the market. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is also zero as a flow; the treasury spends through open on-chain governance rather than a scheduled cliff. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, because no bankruptcy estate or court distribution applies to DOT.

## Buy pressure: where new DOT goes

The buy side is empty. Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is **zero**: Polkadot issues new DOT and never buys it back. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is also **zero**, and this is a genuine change: the old periodic burn of unspent treasury funds was removed in the March 2026 overhaul, and network fees and slashes now route into a shared Dynamic Allocation Pool rather than being destroyed. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — and Buy #4 — new long-term lock — are both zero. With no offset at all, the net read is simply the issuance: about +0.8%.

## Foundation and overhang

DOT has no classic unlock overhang — the token is fully distributed and the early vesting is long expired. The team-controlled supply that exists is the on-chain treasury, funded by its share of issuance and by network revenue routed through the Dynamic Allocation Pool. That treasury is not a stockpile on a release clock; it is spent through public governance votes, which is why no discretionary cliff appears in Sell #3. The framework books no release beyond protocol issuance and re-checks treasury flows on a roughly bi-weekly walk; if a treasury balance falls faster than its governed spend, the outflow enters Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How DOT compares to other uncapped proof-of-stake chains

DOT just moved out of the uncapped class. Until early 2026 it was a continuous-emission proof-of-stake chain with a fixed ~120M DOT yearly mint and no ceiling. The March 2026 change put it under a **2.1 billion DOT** hard cap and a declining issuance schedule — now closer to a halving-model coin than to an open-ended inflation chain. Unlike a fixed-supply token it still mints, but the rate now steps down every two years rather than growing with the network.

The contrast worth drawing is with exchange tokens that burn supply hard enough to go net-deflationary. DOT does the opposite: it has no burn and no buyback, so its only path to lower inflation is the issuance schedule itself. That makes DOT a clean "dilution slows on a clock" case — mildly inflationary today at about **+0.8%** for 90 days, with the rate set to keep falling toward the cap over the coming years.
