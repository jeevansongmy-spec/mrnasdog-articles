## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending June 24 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **HTX at −1.2% net** on the forward view, driven entirely by the quarterly buyback-and-burn with nothing on the sell side to offset it. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day change at **−0.92%**, versus the framework's **−1.20%** burn-driven read for the same window — a gap of about **0.3 percentage points**, comfortably inside tolerance, so the page ships **no monitor-gap chip**. The small difference is that the 10.83T burned on April 15 2026 removed a little more than the monitor's ~8.4T net float decline, because part of the burn drew on supply already outside the active float. HTX is **deflationary by structural buyback** — a token whose only supply force points down.

## Sell pressure: where new HTX comes from

For HTX, the sell side is empty. Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is **zero**: HTX reached its fixed maximum supply of 999.99T at launch and the contract has no active mint function, so no new HTX is created block by block the way an inflating L1 issues coins. Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is also zero: there is no published dated unlock or vesting cliff reaching the market inside the window. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is zero as a flow; DAO-held allocations exist, but no dated discretionary release is scheduled, so the framework books nothing and keeps the overhang monitored. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, because no bankruptcy estate or court-ordered distribution applies to HTX.

## Buy pressure: where new HTX goes

Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is the only force on the page, at about **10.8T HTX** over the next 90 days. Each quarter Huobi HTX allocates **50%** of its platform revenue to buy HTX on the open market and burn it permanently; burns fire mid-quarter, with the **Apr 15 2026** burn destroying **10.83T HTX** (~$19.22M) and the next burn due **Jul 15 2026**. Because the destination is a burn address, those coins are gone for good. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is zero as a separate line: the fee-funded burn is the quarterly buyback already counted in Buy #1, not a second mechanism. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — is zero, with no discretionary open-market buying outside the published quarterly burn. Buy #4 — new long-term lock — is carried at zero: a quarterly liquidity pledge does burn HTX/TRX pool tokens, but the amount is not separately quantified.

## Foundation and overhang

HTX has no classic vesting overhang — the token is fully issued, with no team or seed cliffs left to release. What it has instead is a large DAO-governed allocation inside the original distribution: developer-grant, ecosystem, partnership and platform-development buckets that the DAO controls. These are not on a published release schedule and have shown no dated outflow, so the framework books them at zero flow and tracks them as a monitored overhang on a roughly bi-weekly walk. The single moving part is the burn itself, which only removes supply. If a DAO-controlled balance were to fall between refreshes, that outflow would enter Sell #3 at the next refresh — but no such release is scheduled today.

## How HTX compares to other exchange-token burns

HTX belongs to the class of **exchange tokens with revenue-funded quarterly buyback-and-burns** — the same structural family as the large centralized-exchange coins that route a slice of trading revenue into open-market repurchases and burns. The defining mechanic is that supply only moves one direction: there is no ongoing mint, so the burn is not fighting fresh issuance the way a buyback on an inflating L1 has to. That makes HTX cleanly deflationary on structure, not just on marketing.

The contrast worth drawing is with capped-but-flat tokens and with inflating chains that pair a burn against emission. A hard-capped token that never burns is flat — neither growing nor shrinking — and an inflating chain with a burn can still net positive if the mint outpaces it. HTX sits at the deflationary end of both comparisons: a fixed cap, no mint, and a real burn tied to exchange revenue. The risk that matters for an inflation lens is not new supply but burn size — the quarterly quantum tracks platform revenue, so a weaker trading quarter shrinks the burn and softens the deflation, while a strong quarter deepens it.

## What to watch in the next 90 days

Watch the **Jul 15 2026** quarterly burn — its size, set by HTX's platform revenue for the quarter, is the single number that decides whether net deflation holds near −1.2% or eases. Watch HTX exchange trading volume and revenue, since the burn is funded by 50% of it, so a stronger quarter deepens the burn. Watch for any DAO governance vote that would deploy a developer-grant or ecosystem allocation, which would be the only way a sell-side flow appears. And watch the cumulative burn total, which crossed 110.32T (about 11% of supply) after the Q1 2026 burn — a slow, steady reduction in the float.
