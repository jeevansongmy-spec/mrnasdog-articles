## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending September 28 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **ASTER at about −0.2% net** on the forward view — supply roughly flat, with a fee-funded buyback now just outpacing a sharply reduced issuance. Over the trailing 90 days the same ledger reads about **−5.1% net**, because the buyback-and-burn program reclaimed roughly **143M ASTER** from the open market in the window. Our supply monitor does not cover ASTER — there is no realized-supply row to cross-check against — so the end-check is deferred and the primary on-chain ledger stands on its own, with no monitor-gap chip. ASTER reads as **structurally near-neutral on the active float**, tilted slightly deflationary by the buyback, with the old high-issuance era now behind it.

## Sell pressure: where new ASTER comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is small, at about **5.9M ASTER** over 90 days. Aster has no block reward; its only continuous issuance is staking rewards of **450,000 ASTER** per weekly epoch, paid to coins locked as veASTER. This replaced a fixed monthly release that was roughly **97% larger** (about 78.4M a month), so ongoing issuance is now a minor force on the float.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is the larger sell line this window, at about **10M ASTER**. The team allocation sits behind a one-year cliff from the September 2025 token launch, which opens around **Sep 17 2026** — inside this window — releasing its first monthly tranche of roughly 10M ASTER, after which the 5% team bucket vests near 10M a month. No other large scheduled cliff lands in the period: airdrop and ecosystem allocations now reach the market only through the staking emission counted above, since the old fixed unlock schedule was retired. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is zero: the treasury allocation is fully locked until a governance vote releases it, and no discretionary release is dated in the window. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero, because no bankruptcy estate or court distribution applies to ASTER.

## Buy pressure: where new ASTER goes

Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is the active offset, at about **22M ASTER** over 90 days. Since **Jun 17 2026**, Aster directs **99% of daily platform fees** into buying ASTER on the open market through the day, settled on-chain to a public wallet at a confirmed pace near **245K a day**. The bought coins are paid to lockers as veASTER rewards rather than destroyed, so they pull supply off the market but can return when lockers unwind — which is why the figure is held to the verified on-chain run-rate. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is carried at zero against circulating supply: an equal amount of ASTER is burned from **reserves** each day (the matching half of the program, driving the cap toward a **3B** target), but those coins sit outside the circulating supply, so the burn shrinks the future overhang rather than removing coins already trading. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — and Buy #4 — new long-term lock — are both zero, with no discretionary open-market buying or new escrow beyond the existing veASTER staking announced in the window.

## Foundation and overhang

ASTER carries a large structural overhang, but it is mostly locked or being actively burned. The treasury allocation (about **7%** of supply, roughly 560M ASTER) is held off-market until a governance vote releases it; the team allocation (about **5%**, 400M ASTER) stays fully locked until the **Sep 17 2026** cliff and is the first bucket the reserve burn destroys. The airdrop and ecosystem allocations sit in a locked address and now reach the market only on the much slower staking-driven schedule after the 97% emission cut. None of these book a discretionary sell value today — each is tracked as scope, not as projected flow. The framework re-checks the public buyback wallet, the burn address, and the unlock schedule on a roughly bi-weekly walk; if a locked treasury or team balance falls between refreshes, the outflow enters Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How ASTER compares to other perpetuals-DEX tokens

ASTER belongs to the class of **fee-generating perpetuals-DEX tokens** — coins whose value capture comes from trading fees recycled into buybacks, not from a hard-capped, halving-model supply curve. Unlike a continuous-emission proof-of-stake L1, ASTER has a fixed maximum supply and is actively burning it down toward 3B; unlike a pure fixed-supply token, its float still receives new coins from staking rewards and a team cliff that is just beginning. The result is a token in transition: the heavy early issuance era is over, and a fee-funded buyback-and-burn has ramped up to lean against dilution.

The closest analogue is a high-volume exchange or perp token that funnels most fee revenue into buybacks. The crucial distinction for an inflation lens is **where the bought coins go and where the burn comes from**. Aster pays its buyback to lockers rather than burning it, and burns a matching amount from non-circulating reserves — so the headline "198%" effect is real for the total cap but only partly reduces the coins already trading. That is why the framework reads ASTER as near-neutral on the active float — the buyback just edges out new supply — even as the total supply shrinks below its 8B launch ceiling.
