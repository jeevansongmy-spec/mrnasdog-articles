## The verdict, in one paragraph

For the 90-day window ending June 16 2026, the MrNasdog Pressure Framework reads **ASTER at +2.6% net** on the forward view — supply still growing, but driven almost entirely by a single dated airdrop claim rather than by ongoing issuance. Our supply monitor reads the realized last-90-day change at **+9.06%**, versus the framework's **+8.29%** read for the same trailing window — a gap of about **0.8 percentage points** that ships a **⚠ monitor-gap chip**. That trailing growth reflects the **old fixed monthly release** plus this period's airdrop claims; the structural break is that Aster replaced its fixed emission with a much smaller staking-only model. ASTER is **still inflationary on the active float**, but the rate is set to fall sharply once the airdrop claim clears.

## Sell pressure: where new ASTER comes from

Sell #1 — protocol inflation — is small, at about **5.85M ASTER** over 90 days. Aster has no block reward; its only continuous issuance is staking rewards of **450,000 ASTER** per weekly cycle, paid to coins locked as veASTER. This replaced a fixed monthly release that was roughly **97% larger**, so ongoing issuance is now a minor force.

Sell #2 — vesting unlocks — is the whole story this window, at about **95M ASTER**. A vested airdrop tranche opened a 30-day claim window on June 9 2026, running through July 9 2026 — the one large scheduled release inside the period. Importantly, the team allocation stays fully locked until September 18 2026, just past the window, so no insider supply hits the market here. Sell #3 — Foundation and unscheduled unlocks — is zero: the treasury allocation is fully locked until a governance vote releases it. Sell #4 — long-term locked or bankruptcy — is zero.

## Buy pressure: where new ASTER goes

Buy #1 — programmatic buyback — is the active offset, booked conservatively near **30M ASTER** over 90 days. Since June 17 2026, Aster directs **99% of daily platform fees** into buying ASTER on the open market through the day, settled on-chain to a public wallet. The bought coins are paid to lockers as rewards rather than destroyed, so they pull supply off the market but can return when lockers unwind — which is why the figure is held conservative. Buy #2 — protocol fee burn — is carried at zero against circulating supply: an equal amount of ASTER is burned from **reserves** each day (the matching half of the program, driving the cap down toward a 3B target), but those coins sit outside the circulating supply, so the burn shrinks the future overhang rather than removing coins already trading. Buy #3 — Foundation buy — and Buy #4 — new long-term lock — are both zero.

## Foundation and overhang

ASTER carries a large structural overhang, but it is mostly locked or being actively burned. The treasury allocation (about **7%** of supply) is held off-market until a governance vote releases it; the team allocation (about **5%**) stays fully locked until September 18 2026 and is the first bucket the reserve burn destroys. The remaining airdrop and ecosystem allocations release on a much slower, staking-driven schedule after the 97% emission cut. None of these book a discretionary sell value today — each is tracked as scope, not as projected flow. The framework re-checks the public buyback wallet, the burn address, and the unlock schedule on a roughly bi-weekly walk; if a locked treasury or team balance falls between refreshes, the outflow enters Sell #3 at the next refresh.

## How ASTER compares to other perpetuals-DEX tokens

ASTER belongs to the class of **fee-generating perpetuals-DEX tokens** — coins whose value capture comes from trading fees recycled into buybacks, not from a hard-capped, halving-model supply curve. Unlike a continuous-emission proof-of-stake L1, ASTER has a fixed maximum supply and is actively burning it down; unlike a pure fixed-supply token, its float still grows because large airdrop and ecosystem allocations are mid-release. The result is a token in transition: heavy early issuance is rolling off, and a fee-funded buyback-and-burn is ramping up to replace dilution with scarcity.

The closest analogue is a high-volume exchange or perp token that funnels most fee revenue into buybacks. The crucial distinction for an inflation lens is **where the bought coins go and where the burn comes from**. Aster pays its buyback to lockers rather than burning it, and burns a matching amount from non-circulating reserves — so the headline "198%" effect is real for the total cap but only partly reduces the coins already trading. That is why the framework still reads ASTER as mildly inflationary on the active float even as the total supply shrinks below its 8B launch ceiling.
